I have accumulated a lot of technical notes over the years.

Some were genuinely useful. Some were so vague that even I could not tell what problem they were supposed to solve a month later. That is a good reminder that writing something down is not the same as making it useful.

## A note should help future-me

That is the first test.

If I come back to a note later, I want it to answer at least one of these questions quickly:

- what was I trying to do?
- what went wrong?
- what fixed it?
- what should I be careful about next time?

If it does not do that, it is probably just decoration.

## Specific beats impressive

The notes I trust most are rarely elegant. They are concrete.

They mention:

- the system involved
- the symptom I saw
- the wrong assumptions I made
- the actual fix
- the reason the fix mattered

That level of detail is usually more valuable than a polished overview.

## Notes are not miniature documentation sites

This is the trap I want to avoid.

When every note starts trying to become a definitive guide, the writing slows down and the useful details get sanded away. I would rather keep a short, honest note that captures one thing clearly than produce a broad article that says very little.

That does not mean long-form writing is bad. It just means that not every observation needs ceremony.

## What I want from this site

SinnaKodern should become the place where these notes live in public when they are worth keeping.

That means I want the archive to favor writing that is:

- tied to real experience
- grounded in a specific problem or lesson
- compact unless depth is actually necessary
- written clearly enough to survive rereading

The point is not to publish more. The point is to publish things that continue to be useful after the moment has passed.

## The standard

Good technical notes do not need to sound authoritative.

They need to be usable.

If a note helps me avoid repeating a mistake, recover context faster, or explain a decision more clearly, it has done its job.

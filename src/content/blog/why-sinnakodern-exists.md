This site exists because I wanted a place that feels more like a working notebook than a polished brochure.

I have spent a long time writing code, fixing systems, learning tools the hard way, and accumulating small lessons that rarely fit inside a CV bullet point or a commit message. SinnaKodern is where those lessons get to stay visible.

## What I want this site to be

At its best, this site should be a mix of:

- a personal engineering notebook
- a place for durable technical notes
- a running record of projects, experiments, and mistakes
- a small archive of opinions about software, tooling, and open source

I am less interested in publishing generic tutorials and more interested in writing things I would actually want to reread six months from now.

## What I expect to write about

Most of the material here will probably orbit the same themes:

- Python
- Django
- tooling and workflow
- backend-heavy engineering
- open source and maintainability
- software craftsmanship in the practical, day-to-day sense

That means fewer “ultimate guides” and more notes from the workshop floor.

## The tone I’m aiming for

I want the writing here to stay:

- practical
- specific
- readable
- opinionated when necessary
- honest about trade-offs and uncertainty

If something sounds like template filler, marketing copy, or borrowed certainty, it probably does not belong here.

## Why the personal angle matters

There are already plenty of sites that explain how frameworks work in the abstract. What is more useful to me — and hopefully to other people too — is the grounded version: what I used, what failed, what I learned, and what I would do differently next time.

That is the standard I want SinnaKodern to grow into.

Not comprehensive. Not corporate. Just useful, real, and recognizably mine.
